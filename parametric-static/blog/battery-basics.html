Battery basics

Batteries are the quiet engine of every vape. Understand a few core terms and habits, and you’ll get better performance, longer life, and safer daily use|without becoming an electrical engineer.

What battery types vapes use #

Most modern devices use rechargeable lithium‑ion cells. You’ll encounter two categories:

  • Built‑in (internal) batteries: Non‑removable packs charged via USB‑C. Simple and tidy.
  • Removable cylindrical cells: High‑drain cells like 18650, 20700, 21700 placed in a battery sled. More flexible and often longer‑lasting.

Stick to what your device is designed for|never improvise sizes or chemistries.

Sizes and chemistries #

Common removable sizes (numbers are diameter × length in mm):

Size Typical capacity range Typical CDR range Notes
18650 2000|3000 mAh 15|25 A Lightweight; common in compact mods
20700 3000|4000 mAh 20|30 A Less common than 21700 today
21700 4000|5000 mAh 20|30 A Great blend of capacity and current

Chemistry labels you might see include INR/NMC and IMR|both are appropriate for high‑drain use when honestly rated. Avoid “ultra‑high capacity” claims that also promise very high amps in small sizes; that combo doesn’t exist.

Specs that matter #

  • Nominal voltage: 3.6|3.7 V per cell (full at ~4.2 V, empty near 3.0 V under light load).
  • Capacity (mAh): How much energy the cell stores. More mAh = longer runtime, but often lower current capability in the same size.
  • CDR (continuous‑discharge rating): The safe, sustained current limit without overheating. Use CDR|not “pulse”|for decisions.
  • Internal resistance / voltage sag: Lower resistance sags less under load, feeling snappier at the same wattage.

Ohm’s law and safe wattage #

You don’t need the full textbook, just the essentials:

I = P ÷ V  (current in amps = watts ÷ battery voltage)
P = V × I  (watts = volts × amps)

In a regulated mod, the board steps voltage up or down. A conservative way to estimate the max safe wattage from your batteries is to use the minimum voltage under load (≈3.2 V), your cells’ CDR, and ~90% efficiency:

Max W per cell ≈ CDR × 3.2 V × 0.9
Dual‑cell series ≈ 2 × (CDR × 3.2 V × 0.9)

Example: With 20 A cells: per‑cell ≈ 20 × 3.2 × 0.9 ≈ 58 W. A dual‑cell series mod ≈ 116 W total. Staying below this keeps battery current within CDR.

For mechanical/unregulated devices, additional knowledge and safety practices are required; this guide focuses on regulated devices most beginners use.

Single vs. dual‑battery mods #

  • Single‑battery: Smaller, lighter, great up to moderate wattage. Expect more frequent charging.
  • Dual‑battery (series): Higher safe wattage ceiling and longer runtime. Use married pairs (same model/age) and rotate together.
  • Parallel setups: Share current between cells but still demand matched, healthy batteries.

Charging smart #

  • Use the supplied cable or a quality, standards‑compliant USB‑C cable and charger.
  • For dual‑battery mods, a reputable external charger improves balancing and longevity.
  • Charge on a hard, non‑flammable surface with airflow. Avoid covering chargers.
  • Unplug when full. Warm is normal; hot is a stop sign|disconnect and inspect.

Handling, storage, and travel #

  • Never carry loose cells with keys or coins. Use a plastic battery case or silicone sleeves.
  • Inspect wraps and the top insulator ring regularly; rewrap immediately if damaged.
  • Avoid extreme temperatures. Don’t leave devices in hot cars or in freezing conditions.
  • For long‑term storage, leave cells at ~30|50% charge in a cool, dry place and check every few months.
  • Airline and venue rules vary|check current policies before travel, and transport spare cells in carry‑on, not in checked baggage.

Choosing the right cell #

  1. Pick a wattage target based on your tank/coil (e.g., 40|60 W for many DL tanks; 10|20 W for MTL).
  2. Back‑solve the current using the rule above. Ensure your chosen cell’s CDR comfortably supports it.
  3. Then optimize capacity (mAh) within that safe current range for longer runtime.
  4. Buy from trusted retailers and known OEM models. Be skeptical of specs that look too good to be true.

Lifespan and when to retire #

  • Typical lifespan is 200|500 full cycles depending on usage and care.
  • Retire and recycle any cell with torn wraps you can’t immediately rewrap, missing top ring, dents, swelling, rust, leaks, or a history of hard short.
  • Noticeable capacity crash or one cell in a pair drifting regularly are signs it’s time to replace.

Quick reference #

Do Don’t Why
Match wattage to your cells’ CDR Rely on “pulse” ratings CDR is the safety limit; pulses are undefined
Use married pairs in dual‑battery mods Mix different brands/ages Imbalances stress cells and boards
Carry spares in a plastic case Carry loose with metal objects Prevents short circuits in pockets/bags
Charge on hard surfaces with airflow Charge under pillows or on couches Heat needs to dissipate safely
Buy from trusted retailers Chase unrealistic specs Counterfeits and rewraps are common
